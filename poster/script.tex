\textbf{RYAN} \textit{(\textasciitilde{}35 sec)}

``Hi, thanks for stopping by. Our project replicates DreamBooth, a technique from Ruiz et al.\ at CVPR 2023. The core problem is this: standard text-to-image models like Stable Diffusion can generate a dog, but they can't generate \textit{your} dog. DreamBooth solves this by fine-tuning the model on just 3 to 5 photos of a specific subject, binding it to a rare-token identifier like `[v]'. From then on, you can prompt the model with `[v] dog on the moon' and it will generate that exact subject in any context. Our goal was to replicate this from scratch on Stable Diffusion 1.5 and verify that we could match the paper's quantitative results.''

\textbf{GORDON} \textit{(\textasciitilde{}40 sec)}

``There are two key ideas that make DreamBooth work. First, you label your instance images as `a [v] dog', where [v] is a rare token with no prior meaning, so the model doesn't get confused by semantic baggage. Second, prior-preservation loss: during fine-tuning, you simultaneously train on pre-generated class images---generic dogs---to prevent language drift, where the model forgets what a dog looks like in general.

Architecturally, SD 1.5 has three components: a VAE for encoding and decoding images, a UNet that does the actual denoising---this is what we fine-tune---and a frozen CLIP text encoder. The combined loss is just the denoising loss on instance images plus lambda times the same loss on class images, where lambda is 1.0. We trained for 800 steps at a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-6}$ using 8-bit AdamW.''

\textbf{CALEB} \textit{(\textasciitilde{}35 sec)}

``Our quantitative results used three subjects from the official DreamBooth dataset: two dogs and a backpack. We evaluated with DINO and CLIP-I for subject fidelity, and CLIP-T for prompt fidelity. Our mean DINO was 0.598, CLIP-I was 0.861, and CLIP-T was 0.265---all within or very close to the expected SD 1.5 range from the paper.

Beyond the main replication, we explored two extensions: fine-tuning on brand logos to test how well the method handles sharp edges and flat colors, and art style transfer to transfer a specific artist's visual style rather than a physical subject.''

\textbf{EVAN} \textit{(\textasciitilde{}30 sec)}

``A few key engineering lessons stood out. Prior preservation is non-negotiable---without it, the model collapses entirely to the subject. We also found that fp32 precision for the UNet is critical; fp16 gradients underflow and break identity binding entirely. And importantly, fine-tuning only the UNet and keeping CLIP frozen gave better results than unfreezing both---jointly fine-tuning the text encoder led to overfitting.

The whole pipeline runs in 30 to 40 minutes on a free Colab T4, which makes it surprisingly accessible. We're happy to take any questions about the training loop, the metrics, or the extensions.''
